# Space Technology and Human Spaceflight Knowledge Base

Compact, retrieval-friendly notes for the VR astronomy assistant.

## Rockets and Launch Vehicles

### How Rockets Work
- Rockets work by Newton's Third Law: expelling mass (exhaust) backward propels the rocket forward.
- Chemical rockets burn propellant (fuel + oxidizer) to create hot gases.
- Specific impulse (Isp): measure of rocket engine efficiency (seconds); higher = more efficient.
- Stages: most rockets use multiple stages; each stage is discarded when its fuel is exhausted.
- Escape velocity from Earth's surface: ~11.2 km/s (~40,320 km/h).

### Current Major Launch Vehicles (2025-2026)
- SpaceX Falcon 9: workhorse rocket; reusable first stage; over 300 successful launches.
- SpaceX Falcon Heavy: most powerful operational rocket until SLS; 3 Falcon 9 cores strapped together.
- SpaceX Starship: largest and most powerful rocket ever built; fully reusable design; 33 Raptor engines on Super Heavy booster.
- NASA SLS (Space Launch System): super heavy-lift for Artemis missions; expendable.
- ULA Vulcan Centaur: successor to Atlas V and Delta IV; BE-4 engines.
- Ariane 6 (ESA): European heavy-lift launcher; replaced Ariane 5.
- Long March 5 (China): heavy-lift for Chinese space station and lunar missions.

## Space Stations

### International Space Station (ISS)
- Largest human-made structure in space: ~109m × 73m (football field size).
- Orbits at ~408 km altitude; completes one orbit every ~90 minutes.
- Continuous human occupation since November 2000 (25+ years).
- 16 pressurized modules from 5 space agencies (NASA, Roscosmos, ESA, JAXA, CSA).
- Research areas: microgravity science, biology, materials, Earth observation.
- Planned retirement: around 2030; commercial stations expected to replace it.

### Tiangong (Chinese Space Station)
- China's modular space station; operational since 2022.
- Three main modules: Tianhe (core), Wentian (lab), Mengtian (lab).
- Supports 3-person crew; 6 during rotations.

### Commercial Space Stations (planned)
- Axiom Station: modules attaching to ISS initially, then free-flying.
- Orbital Reef (Blue Origin + Sierra Space): planned commercial station.
- Starlab (Voyager Space + Airbus): planned commercial station.

## Human Spaceflight Milestones
- 1961: Yuri Gagarin (USSR) — first human in space (Vostok 1, April 12).
- 1961: Alan Shepard (USA) — first American in space (Freedom 7, May 5).
- 1962: John Glenn (USA) — first American to orbit Earth (Friendship 7).
- 1963: Valentina Tereshkova (USSR) — first woman in space (Vostok 6).
- 1965: Alexei Leonov (USSR) — first spacewalk (Voskhod 2).
- 1969: Neil Armstrong & Buzz Aldrin (USA) — first humans on the Moon (Apollo 11, July 20).
- 1970: Apollo 13 — successful failure; crew returned safely after oxygen tank explosion.
- 1981-2011: Space Shuttle program (135 missions).
- 1998-2011: ISS assembly.
- 2020: SpaceX Crew Dragon — first commercial crew vehicle to ISS (Demo-2).
- 2022: Artemis I — first SLS/Orion flight test (uncrewed).

## Spacewalks (EVA)
- EVA: Extra-Vehicular Activity.
- First EVA: Alexei Leonov, 1965 (12 minutes outside Voskhod 2).
- First American EVA: Ed White, 1965 (Gemini 4).
- Total ISS EVAs: over 260 as of 2025.
- Modern EVA suits: NASA's xEMU (Exploration Extravehicular Mobility Unit) for Artemis; Axiom Space suit for lunar surface.
- EVA duration: typically 6-8 hours.

## Living in Space
- Microgravity effects on the body:
  - Bone density loss: ~1-2% per month without exercise.
  - Muscle atrophy: significant loss without resistance training.
  - Fluid redistribution: "puffy face, bird legs" syndrome.
  - Vision changes: some astronauts experience vision deterioration (SANS).
  - Height increase: ~2 cm due to spinal decompression.
- Countermeasures: 2+ hours of exercise daily on ISS (treadmill, cycle, resistance device).
- Food: freeze-dried, thermostabilized; some fresh food delivered by cargo missions.
- Sleep: astronauts sleep in sleeping bags attached to walls; ~8.5 hours scheduled.
- Radiation: ISS crew receives ~150 mSv/year (vs ~3 mSv/year on Earth).

## Planetary Defense
- Near-Earth Objects (NEOs): asteroids/comets with orbits close to Earth's.
- Potentially Hazardous Asteroids (PHAs): NEOs >140m that pass within 7.5 million km.
- NASA tracks ~34,000 NEOs; ~2,400 classified as PHAs.
- DART mission (2022): NASA deliberately impacted asteroid Dimorphos, successfully changing its orbit — first planetary defense test.
- ESA Hera mission: follow-up to DART; will study Dimorphos impact results in detail.
- Planetary defense methods: kinetic impactor (DART), gravity tractor, nuclear deflection.

## Space Weather
- Solar wind: continuous stream of charged particles from the Sun.
- Solar flares: sudden eruptions of energy from the Sun's surface.
- Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs): large expulsions of plasma and magnetic field.
- Effects on Earth: aurora (Northern/Southern Lights), satellite disruptions, power grid issues.
- Solar cycle: ~11 year cycle of solar activity (sunspot minimum to maximum).
- Current solar cycle 25 expected to peak around 2025-2026.

## Future of Space Exploration
- Moon: Artemis program building toward sustained lunar presence; Gateway station in lunar orbit.
- Mars: NASA's Moon-to-Mars roadmap; SpaceX Starship for Mars transport.
- Asteroid mining: potential for extracting water, metals, and rare minerals.
- Space tourism: suborbital (Blue Origin New Shepard, Virgin Galactic) and orbital (SpaceX, Axiom).
- Interstellar probes: Breakthrough Starshot concept for laser-propelled nanocraft to Alpha Centauri.
