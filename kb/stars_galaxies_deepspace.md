# Stars, Galaxies, and Deep Space Knowledge Base

Compact, retrieval-friendly notes for the VR astronomy assistant.

## Stars — Basics
- Stars are massive balls of hot gas (plasma) powered by nuclear fusion in their cores.
- Main fuel: hydrogen fused into helium (proton-proton chain or CNO cycle).
- Stars form from collapsing clouds of gas and dust called nebulae.
- A star's mass determines its color, temperature, luminosity, and lifespan.
- Star classification (spectral types, hottest to coolest): O, B, A, F, G, K, M.
  - Mnemonic: "Oh Be A Fine Girl/Guy, Kiss Me".
  - O-type: blue, >30,000 K, very massive, short-lived (~millions of years).
  - M-type: red, <3,500 K, least massive, long-lived (~trillions of years).
  - The Sun is a G2V star (~5,778 K surface temperature).

## Star Life Cycle
- Nebula → Protostar → Main Sequence → (depending on mass):
  - Low/medium mass (like the Sun): Red Giant → Planetary Nebula → White Dwarf.
  - High mass: Red Supergiant → Supernova → Neutron Star or Black Hole.
- Main sequence: the longest phase; star is in equilibrium (gravity vs fusion pressure).
- Red giant: outer layers expand as core contracts; star cools and reddens.
- Supernova: catastrophic explosion of massive star; can outshine entire galaxy briefly.
- White dwarf: Earth-sized remnant of low/medium mass star; gradually cools over billions of years.
- Neutron star: incredibly dense remnant (~1.4 solar masses in ~20 km diameter); a teaspoon would weigh ~6 billion tons.
- Pulsar: rapidly rotating neutron star emitting beams of radiation.

## Black Holes
- Formed when massive stars collapse and gravity becomes so strong that nothing, not even light, can escape.
- Event horizon: the boundary beyond which escape is impossible.
- Schwarzschild radius: radius of event horizon for non-rotating black hole.
- Types:
  - Stellar black holes: 3–100 solar masses; formed from supernova remnants.
  - Supermassive black holes: millions to billions of solar masses; found at centers of most galaxies.
  - Intermediate black holes: hundreds to thousands of solar masses; rare, evidence growing.
- Sagittarius A* (Sgr A*): the supermassive black hole at the center of our Milky Way; ~4 million solar masses.
- M87*: first black hole ever photographed (Event Horizon Telescope, 2019); ~6.5 billion solar masses.
- Black holes don't "suck in" everything — objects can orbit them safely at a distance.
- Hawking radiation: theoretical prediction that black holes slowly evaporate over enormous timescales.

## Galaxies
- A galaxy is a gravitationally bound system of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter.
- Types (Hubble classification):
  - Spiral: disk-shaped with spiral arms (e.g., Milky Way, Andromeda).
  - Elliptical: round/oval, mostly older stars, little gas/dust.
  - Irregular: no defined shape (e.g., Large/Small Magellanic Clouds).
  - Lenticular: disk-shaped but no spiral arms.

## The Milky Way
- Our home galaxy; barred spiral galaxy.
- Diameter: ~100,000 light-years.
- Contains 100–400 billion stars.
- The Sun is located ~26,000 light-years from the galactic center, in the Orion Arm.
- One full orbit around the galactic center takes ~225–250 million years (a "galactic year").
- Central supermassive black hole: Sagittarius A*.

## Andromeda Galaxy (M31)
- Nearest large spiral galaxy to the Milky Way.
- Distance: ~2.5 million light-years.
- Larger than the Milky Way: ~220,000 light-years diameter.
- On a collision course with the Milky Way; merger expected in ~4.5 billion years ("Milkomeda").

## Nebulae
- Clouds of gas and dust in space; can be sites of star formation or remnants of dead stars.
- Types:
  - Emission nebulae: glow from ionized gas (e.g., Orion Nebula).
  - Reflection nebulae: reflect light from nearby stars.
  - Planetary nebulae: expanding shells from dying low-mass stars.
  - Supernova remnants: debris from stellar explosions (e.g., Crab Nebula).
  - Dark nebulae: dense clouds blocking background light (e.g., Horsehead Nebula).
- Orion Nebula (M42): one of the brightest nebulae; visible to the naked eye; active star-forming region ~1,344 light-years away.
- Pillars of Creation: famous star-forming columns in the Eagle Nebula, imaged by Hubble (1995) and JWST (2022).

## Constellations (popular ones)
- Orion: "The Hunter"; contains Betelgeuse (red supergiant) and Rigel (blue supergiant); Orion Nebula in sword.
- Ursa Major: "Great Bear"; contains the Big Dipper asterism.
- Ursa Minor: "Little Bear"; contains Polaris (North Star).
- Cassiopeia: W-shaped; visible year-round in Northern Hemisphere.
- Scorpius: contains Antares (red supergiant); prominent in summer sky.
- Southern Cross (Crux): key navigation constellation in Southern Hemisphere.

## Exoplanets
- Planets orbiting stars other than the Sun.
- Over 5,700 confirmed exoplanets as of 2026.
- Detection methods: transit method (most common), radial velocity, direct imaging, gravitational microlensing.
- Kepler Space Telescope: discovered thousands of exoplanets (2009–2018).
- TESS (Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite): ongoing survey of nearby bright stars.
- James Webb Space Telescope (JWST): can analyze exoplanet atmospheres for biosignatures.
- Habitable zone ("Goldilocks zone"): orbital region where liquid water could exist on a planet's surface.
- TRAPPIST-1 system: 7 Earth-sized planets; 3 in habitable zone; ~40 light-years away.
- Proxima Centauri b: nearest known exoplanet; ~4.2 light-years; in habitable zone.

## Space Telescopes
- Hubble Space Telescope (HST): launched 1990; optical/UV/near-IR; revolutionized astronomy.
- James Webb Space Telescope (JWST): launched Dec 2021; infrared; largest space telescope ever; orbits L2 point ~1.5 million km from Earth.
- Chandra X-ray Observatory: launched 1999; X-ray astronomy.
- Spitzer Space Telescope: infrared; operated 2003–2020.
- Roman Space Telescope (Nancy Grace Roman): planned launch late 2020s; wide-field infrared surveys.

## Cosmology (Big Picture)
- The universe is ~13.8 billion years old (since the Big Bang).
- The observable universe is ~93 billion light-years in diameter.
- The universe is expanding; expansion is accelerating (discovered 1998; Nobel Prize 2011).
- Dark matter: ~27% of universe; doesn't emit light; detected via gravitational effects.
- Dark energy: ~68% of universe; drives accelerating expansion; nature unknown.
- Ordinary (baryonic) matter: only ~5% of the universe.
- Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB): afterglow of the Big Bang; discovered 1965; mapped by COBE, WMAP, Planck.

## Fun Space Facts
- A day on Venus is longer than a year on Venus (243 Earth days rotation vs 225 days orbit).
- Neutron stars can spin up to 716 times per second.
- Space is completely silent — no medium for sound waves to travel through.
- The footprints on the Moon will last millions of years (no wind or water erosion).
- One million Earths could fit inside the Sun.
- Light from the nearest star system (Alpha Centauri) takes ~4.37 years to reach Earth.
- The International Space Station orbits Earth every ~90 minutes at ~28,000 km/h.
- Astronauts grow ~2 cm taller in space (spinal decompression in microgravity).
