# Space Missions Knowledge Base

Use this local knowledge base to keep the assistant *space-mission focused*.
This file is intentionally written as concise notes for retrieval (RAG). Dates and schedules can change.

## What is a space mission?
- A space mission is an organized effort to explore, observe, or operate in space using robotic spacecraft, human crews, or both.
- Common goals: science discovery, technology demonstration, Earth observation, communications/navigation services, and building capability for sustained exploration.

## Main mission categories
- Earth observation: climate/weather monitoring, mapping, disaster response.
- Science & exploration: planetary probes, telescopes, landers/rovers, sample-return.
- Telecom & navigation: communications relays, GNSS positioning, timing.
- Human spaceflight: crewed transport, space stations, lunar exploration.

## Key objectives (typical)
- Characterize destinations (Moon/Mars/asteroids/icy moons): geology, composition, environment.
- Develop deep-space capabilities: life support, radiation mitigation, power, autonomy, comms.
- Use LEO platforms (e.g., ISS or commercial stations) for long-duration human operations and systems testing.
- Strengthen international + commercial partnerships for sustainability and cadence.

## Apollo (NASA, legacy human lunar program)
- Goal: land humans on the Moon and return them safely.
- Architecture: Saturn V + Command Module (crew) + Lunar Module (landing).
- Apollo 11 (1969) first crewed lunar landing; later missions extended surface operations and science return.

## Artemis (NASA, modern lunar program)
- Goal: return humans to the Moon and build sustained lunar exploration infrastructure and experience.
- Emphasis: modern systems, longer-duration operations, and a pathway to future deep-space missions.

## Notable missions and themes (mid/late-2020s)
Note: Specific launch/arrival dates are subject to schedule changes.

- Artemis II (NASA, planned): crewed lunar flyby-style mission intended to return astronauts to lunar vicinity after decades.
- Europa Clipper (NASA): mission to study Jupiter’s moon Europa with repeated flybys and remote sensing to assess habitability-related questions.
- OSIRIS-APEX (NASA): follow-on mission to observe asteroid Apophis around its close Earth encounter timeframe and study physical changes.
- Axiom missions (commercial): private crewed missions to low Earth orbit and the ISS, building experience and market pathways toward commercial stations.

## Space exploration timeline (high-level)
- Early motivation: humans have long been inspired by the night sky; practical exploration required rockets capable of reaching orbital velocity.
- 1930s–1940s: long-range rocketry advanced rapidly during WWII; Germany’s V-2 demonstrated high-altitude ballistic flight, and post-war the US/USSR accelerated missile/rocket programs.
- 1957: Sputnik 1 (USSR) became the first artificial satellite, triggering an intense space-race era.
- 1961: Yuri Gagarin (Vostok 1) became the first human to orbit Earth.
- Early US milestones: Explorer 1 was the first US satellite; Alan Shepard was the first American in space (suborbital); John Glenn was the first American to orbit Earth.

### Moon landing era
- 1960s: robotic probes and lunar photography/scouting preceded crewed lunar landings.
- Apollo program: national goal set in the early 1960s to land humans on the Moon and return them safely; six crewed lunar landings occurred between 1969 and 1972.
- Apollo 12 (1969) was the second crewed lunar landing.

### Robotic exploration and satellites (1970s)
- Communications/navigation satellites became routine infrastructure.
- Mars: Mariner missions advanced mapping/planetary science.
- Outer planets: Voyager returned detailed observations of Jupiter/Saturn systems (rings and moons).
- Human spaceflight: Skylab (first US space station) and the Apollo–Soyuz Test Project (early US–USSR cooperation).

### Space Shuttle era (1981–2011)
- The Space Shuttle was a reusable crewed spacecraft used for research, satellite deployment/servicing, and ISS assembly.
- Major losses: Challenger (1986) and Columbia (2003), each followed by long suspensions and major safety investigations.
- Program end: orbiters were retired in 2011, closing the Shuttle era.

### Satellites and modern operations
- Satellites became central to communications, navigation, remote sensing, weather, and security.
- Modern conflicts highlighted the strategic value of space-based reconnaissance, timing, and positioning.

### International Space Station (ISS)
- The ISS is a long-duration research laboratory in LEO and a major international partnership.
- Continuous human presence began in 2000; it is serviced by multiple visiting vehicles (e.g., Soyuz/Progress and modern commercial cargo/crew spacecraft).

### Future focus (Moon-to-Mars themes)
- Reducing launch costs and improving reliability continues via new launch systems and commercial competition.
- Mars remains a major long-term target; robotic orbiters/landers/rovers mature technologies and improve environmental knowledge.
- Key research themes include radiation measurement/mitigation and in-situ resource utilization (ISRU), including oxygen production experiments.

## Common terms / abbreviations
- EVA: Extra-Vehicular Activity (spacewalk).
- LEO: Low Earth Orbit.
- TLI: Translunar Injection (burn to leave Earth orbit toward the Moon).
- LOI: Lunar Orbit Insertion.
