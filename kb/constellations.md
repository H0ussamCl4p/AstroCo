# Constellations & Night Sky Guide

Retrieval-friendly notes for the VR astronomy assistant about constellations and stargazing.

## What Are Constellations?
- Constellations are patterns of stars as seen from Earth, grouped by ancient cultures into recognizable shapes.
- The International Astronomical Union (IAU) officially recognizes 88 constellations covering the entire sky.
- Stars in a constellation are usually not physically close; they appear grouped from our perspective.
- Constellations are useful for navigation, storytelling, and locating celestial objects.

## Major Northern Constellations

### Ursa Major (Great Bear)
- Contains the Big Dipper asterism (7 bright stars forming a ladle/plough shape).
- The two "pointer stars" (Dubhe and Merak) point toward Polaris, the North Star.
- Visible year-round from most northern latitudes.
- In many cultures: a bear, a wagon, a plough, or seven sages.

### Ursa Minor (Little Bear)
- Contains the Little Dipper and Polaris (the North Star) at the tip of its tail.
- Polaris is currently within 1° of the true north celestial pole.
- Polaris is not the brightest star in the sky — it ranks about 48th.
- Due to Earth's precession, a different star will be the "north star" in ~12,000 years (Vega).

### Orion (The Hunter)
- One of the most recognizable constellations worldwide.
- Betelgeuse: red supergiant in Orion's shoulder; ~700 light-years away; will eventually go supernova.
- Rigel: blue supergiant in Orion's foot; ~860 light-years away; one of the brightest stars visible.
- Orion's Belt: three stars in a row (Alnitak, Alnilam, Mintaka).
- Orion Nebula (M42): visible to naked eye below the belt; a stellar nursery ~1,344 light-years away.
- Best visible in winter (Northern Hemisphere).

### Cassiopeia
- W-shaped or M-shaped pattern of 5 bright stars.
- Named after a vain queen in Greek mythology.
- Circumpolar (never sets) from most northern latitudes.
- Contains several open star clusters visible with binoculars.

### Leo (The Lion)
- Spring constellation; recognizable by the "sickle" or backward question mark shape.
- Regulus: brightest star, ~79 light-years away.
- Associated with the Nemean Lion from Greek mythology.

## Major Southern Constellations

### Crux (Southern Cross)
- Smallest of all 88 constellations but very prominent in the southern sky.
- Used for navigation: the long axis points roughly toward the south celestial pole.
- Featured on flags of Australia, New Zealand, Brazil, and other southern nations.
- Contains the Jewel Box Cluster (NGC 4755) and the Coalsack Nebula (dark nebula).

### Centaurus
- One of the largest constellations.
- Contains Alpha Centauri, the closest star system to the Sun at 4.37 light-years.
- Alpha Centauri is actually a triple star system (A, B, and Proxima Centauri).
- Proxima Centauri has at least one confirmed exoplanet (Proxima Centauri b) in the habitable zone.

### Scorpius (The Scorpion)
- Summer constellation (Northern Hemisphere); prominent in southern skies.
- Antares: red supergiant "heart of the scorpion"; ~550 light-years away.
- Rich in star clusters and nebulae due to its location near the galactic center.

## Zodiac Constellations
- 12 constellations along the ecliptic (the Sun's apparent path): Aries, Taurus, Gemini, Cancer, Leo, Virgo, Libra, Scorpius, Sagittarius, Capricornus, Aquarius, Pisces.
- The Sun, Moon, and planets appear to move through these constellations.
- Ophiuchus (the Serpent Bearer) also lies on the ecliptic but is not a traditional zodiac sign.
- Due to Earth's precession, the astronomical positions have shifted from the astrological dates.

## Famous Stars

### Sirius (Dog Star)
- Brightest star in Earth's night sky (apparent magnitude −1.46).
- Located in Canis Major, ~8.6 light-years away.
- Actually a binary system: Sirius A (main star) and Sirius B (white dwarf).
- Important to ancient Egyptians: its rising marked the annual Nile flood.

### Vega
- In the constellation Lyra; ~25 light-years away.
- One of the brightest stars in northern sky.
- Was the north star ~12,000 years ago and will be again in ~12,000 years.
- First star (other than the Sun) to be photographed (1850).

### Polaris (North Star)
- Currently the closest bright star to the north celestial pole.
- A Cepheid variable star (its brightness pulsates).
- Actually a triple star system.
- ~430 light-years from Earth.

## Stargazing Tips
- Dark adaptation: let your eyes adjust for 20-30 minutes without bright lights.
- Use red light to preserve night vision.
- Binoculars are often better than telescopes for beginners — wider field of view.
- Best stargazing: away from city lights, clear sky, no moon (new moon phase).
- Look for the Milky Way band in summer from dark locations — you're seeing the disk of our galaxy edge-on.
- Satellites and the ISS are visible as steady, moving "stars" — ISS is the 3rd brightest object in the night sky.
